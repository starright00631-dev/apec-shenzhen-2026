Why Shenzhen, Why Now

The Message Behind the Choice

In 1980, Shenzhen was a fishing village of 30,000 people. Deng Xiaoping chose it as China’s first Special Economic Zone with a famous remark that would come to define the city’s entire ethos: “I don’t care if it’s black or white, a cat that catches mice is a good cat.” This pragmatism — results over ideology, experimentation over orthodoxy — built the city that now houses Tencent, Huawei, DJI, and BYD. It built the city that files more international patents than any single city on Earth, that electrified its entire 16,000-bus fleet before any other megacity dared, that somehow found room for 1,200 parks inside the densest urban landscape in China.

China chose Shenzhen for APEC 2026 to send a message: the country’s next chapter is about innovation, openness, and the kind of economic experimentation that Shenzhen embodies.

Shenzhen’s neighborhoods each tell a different chapter of this story — from the tech laboratories of Nanshan to the street-food chaos of Luohu, from the convention-center polish of Futian to the ancient Hakka villages of Dapeng. Explore them on the city guide.

Shenzhen’s multilingual service centres are prepared for APEC 2026 arrivals.

This is not a city chosen for its skyline alone. Shenzhen was selected because its story is APEC’s story — the idea that economies grow fastest when they open up, when they let pragmatism guide policy, when they build bridges instead of walls. From Shanghai in 2001 to Beijing in 2014, each Chinese host city marked a different phase of the country’s economic evolution. Shenzhen marks the next one: the shift from manufacturing scale to innovation depth, from export-driven growth to technology leadership.

Shenzhen’s Story

A City That Rewrote the Rules

Three moments that made Shenzhen what it is — told not as statistics, but as turning points.

Shenzhen skyline at dusk

The Experiment

In August 1980, Deng Xiaoping designated a stretch of farmland bordering Hong Kong as China’s first Special Economic Zone. The logic was almost absurd in its simplicity: put a market-oriented sandbox next to the world’s freest economy and see what happens. What happened was construction at a pace the world had never seen. Early entrepreneurs — many of them migrant workers sleeping in factory dormitories — literally built the city overnight, brick by brick, shifting 100 million cubic metres of earth to flatten hills for factories that would soon supply the world’s electronics.

Shenzhen’s innovation and tech scene

The Hardware Revolution

Huaqiangbei, a single square kilometre in Futian, became the electronics capital of the world — a place where you could walk in with a sketch on a napkin and walk out with a working prototype the same afternoon. It was the soil from which Shenzhen’s hardware giants grew. A young man named Wang Jian started Ubtech Robotics here in 2012; a decade later, his company manufactures humanoid robots used in education and healthcare across 40 countries. DJI, founded by a PhD student in a small office in Nanshan, now controls over 70% of the global consumer drone market from this same city — the kind of rapid scaling that APEC’s digital economy agenda aims to enable across all 21 member economies.

Shenzhen’s ecological green spaces

The Green Megacity

The same city that once choked on construction dust made a quiet, startling decision: it would become the greenest megacity in China. In 2017, Shenzhen completed the full electrification of its 16,000-bus fleet — the first megacity on Earth to do so. Today, 45% of the city’s total area is green space. You can ride an electric bus from the financial district to the Futian Mangrove Reserve in under 30 minutes, stepping from glass towers into one of the last intact mangrove ecosystems on China’s coast, where black-faced spoonbills pause during their annual migration along the East Asian-Australasian flyway.
